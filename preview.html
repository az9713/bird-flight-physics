Physics of Bird Wing Flapping

A coherent mathematical derivation with all symbols defined before use.

wing motion $\rightarrow$ relative airflow $\rightarrow$ pressure/circulation $\rightarrow$ force $\rightarrow$ wake momentum/vorticity $\rightarrow$ power cost

0. The central problem

A bird in steady level flight must support its weight and generate thrust to overcome drag.

$m$ = bird mass.

$g$ = gravitational acceleration.

$W = mg$ = bird weight.

$U$ = forward flight speed relative to still air.

$D$ = total aerodynamic drag on the bird.

$T$ = wingbeat period.

$F_z(t)$ = instantaneous vertical aerodynamic force.

$F_x(t)$ = instantaneous forward aerodynamic force.

The average of any time-dependent quantity $F(t)$ over one wingbeat cycle is

$$ \langle F\rangle = \frac{1}{T}\int_0^T F(t)\,dt. $$

For steady level flight, the cycle-averaged force balances are

$$ \langle F_z\rangle = W = mg, $$ $$ \langle F_x\rangle = D. $$
The wingbeat must create enough vertical force to balance weight and enough forward force to overcome drag.

1. The fluid-mechanical problem

The air is modeled as an incompressible viscous fluid.

$\mathbf{x} = (x,y,z)$ = position vector in space.

$x$ = forward direction.

$y$ = spanwise direction.

$z$ = upward direction.

$t$ = time.

$\mathbf{u}(\mathbf{x},t)$ = air velocity field.

$p(\mathbf{x},t)$ = pressure field.

$\rho$ = air density.

$\mu$ = dynamic viscosity of air.

$\nu = \mu/\rho$ = kinematic viscosity of air.

The governing equations are the incompressible Navier–Stokes equations:

$$ \nabla\cdot \mathbf{u}=0, $$ $$ \rho\left(\frac{\partial \mathbf{u}}{\partial t}+\mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla \mathbf{u}\right) = -\nabla p + \mu \nabla^2\mathbf{u}. $$

The first equation enforces incompressibility. The second equation is Newton's second law applied to a moving fluid element.

$S_w(t)$ = instantaneous moving wing surface.

$\mathbf{V}_w(\mathbf{x},t)$ = velocity of a point on the wing surface.

The no-slip boundary condition on the moving wing is

$$ \mathbf{u}(\mathbf{x},t)=\mathbf{V}_w(\mathbf{x},t), \qquad \mathbf{x}\in S_w(t). $$

The wing therefore imposes motion on the surrounding air.

2. Force from pressure and shear

$\mathbf{n}$ = unit normal vector pointing from the wing into the air.

$\boldsymbol{\sigma}$ = fluid stress tensor.

$\mathbf{I}$ = identity tensor.

For a Newtonian fluid,

$$ \boldsymbol{\sigma} = -p\mathbf{I} + \mu\left(\nabla\mathbf{u}+\nabla\mathbf{u}^{T}\right). $$

The aerodynamic force on the wing is

$$ \mathbf{F}(t) = \int_{S_w(t)}\boldsymbol{\sigma}\mathbf{n}\,dS. $$

Substituting the stress tensor gives

$$ \mathbf{F}(t) = \int_{S_w(t)} \left[-p\mathbf{I}+\mu\left(\nabla\mathbf{u}+\nabla\mathbf{u}^{T}\right)\right] \mathbf{n}\,dS. $$

The pressure contribution is

$$ \mathbf{F}_p(t)=-\int_{S_w(t)}p\mathbf{n}\,dS. $$

The viscous shear contribution is

$$ \mathbf{F}_{\mu}(t) = \int_{S_w(t)} \mu\left(\nabla\mathbf{u}+\nabla\mathbf{u}^{T}\right)\mathbf{n}\,dS. $$

For bird-sized flight, pressure forces usually dominate, so

$$ \mathbf{F}(t)\approx \mathbf{F}_p(t). $$
At leading order, bird flight is pressure engineering produced by time-dependent wing motion.

3. Wing geometry and kinematics

$b$ = span of one wing, measured from shoulder to wingtip.

$r$ = spanwise coordinate, with $0\le r\le b$.

$c(r)$ = local chord length at spanwise position $r$.

$S$ = total area of both wings.

The wing area is approximately

$$ S\approx 2\int_0^b c(r)\,dr. $$

$h(r,t)$ = vertical displacement of the wing section at span $r$.

$\theta(r,t)$ = local pitch angle of that wing section.

$H(r)$ = vertical flapping amplitude at span $r$.

$f$ = flapping frequency.

$\omega = 2\pi f$ = angular flapping frequency.

A simple periodic vertical flapping model is

$$ h(r,t)=H(r)\sin(\omega t). $$

The vertical velocity is

$$ \dot h(r,t)=\frac{\partial h}{\partial t}=\omega H(r)\cos(\omega t). $$

The vertical acceleration is

$$ \ddot h(r,t)=\frac{\partial^2 h}{\partial t^2}=-\omega^2H(r)\sin(\omega t). $$

$\theta_0(r)$ = mean pitch angle.

$\Theta(r)$ = pitch oscillation amplitude.

$\phi$ = phase offset between vertical flapping and pitching.

A simple periodic pitch model is

$$ \theta(r,t)=\theta_0(r)+\Theta(r)\sin(\omega t+\phi). $$

The phase $\phi$ is dynamically important because it controls whether high lift occurs when the force is tilted in a thrust-producing direction.

4. Relative airflow and effective angle of attack

$\mathbf{V}_{\mathrm{rel}}(r,t)$ = local airflow velocity relative to the moving wing section.

$U_{\mathrm{eff}}(r,t)$ = magnitude of the relative airflow.

$\gamma(r,t)$ = inflow angle caused by vertical flapping motion.

$\alpha_{\mathrm{eff}}(r,t)$ = effective angle of attack.

In a two-dimensional approximation, the wing section sees a forward component $U$ and a vertical component $\dot h(r,t)$. Hence

$$ U_{\mathrm{eff}}(r,t)=\sqrt{U^2+\dot h(r,t)^2}. $$

The relative wind is tilted by

$$ \gamma(r,t)=\tan^{-1}\left(\frac{\dot h(r,t)}{U}\right). $$

The effective angle of attack is therefore

$$ \alpha_{\mathrm{eff}}(r,t)=\theta(r,t)-\gamma(r,t), $$ or explicitly, $$ \alpha_{\mathrm{eff}}(r,t) =\theta(r,t)-\tan^{-1}\left(\frac{\dot h(r,t)}{U}\right). $$

For small $\dot h/U$,

$$ \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{\dot h}{U}\right)\approx \frac{\dot h}{U}, $$ so $$ \alpha_{\mathrm{eff}}(r,t)\approx \theta(r,t)-\frac{\dot h(r,t)}{U}. $$
Flapping does not merely move the wing up and down. It changes the effective angle of attack, and therefore changes circulation, pressure, lift, drag, and thrust.

5. Local lift and drag on a wing section

$L'(r,t)$ = lift force per unit span at position $r$.

$D'(r,t)$ = drag force per unit span at position $r$.

$C_L(\alpha_{\mathrm{eff}})$ = lift coefficient.

$C_D(\alpha_{\mathrm{eff}})$ = drag coefficient.

A quasi-steady sectional model gives

$$ L'(r,t)=\frac12\rho U_{\mathrm{eff}}(r,t)^2 c(r) C_L\!\left(\alpha_{\mathrm{eff}}(r,t)\right), $$ $$ D'(r,t)=\frac12\rho U_{\mathrm{eff}}(r,t)^2 c(r) C_D\!\left(\alpha_{\mathrm{eff}}(r,t)\right). $$

For small angles and attached flow, thin-airfoil theory gives

$$ C_L(\alpha_{\mathrm{eff}})\approx 2\pi\alpha_{\mathrm{eff}}, $$ where $\alpha_{\mathrm{eff}}$ is measured in radians.

Therefore,

$$ L'(r,t)\approx \pi\rho U_{\mathrm{eff}}(r,t)^2 c(r)\alpha_{\mathrm{eff}}(r,t). $$

This attached-flow formula is not valid during strong stall or strong leading-edge-vortex phases, but it is the cleanest first approximation.

6. From sectional forces to whole-bird force

The total lift from one wing is

$$ L_{\text{one wing}}(t)=\int_0^b L'(r,t)\,dr. $$

For two wings,

$$ L(t)=2\int_0^b L'(r,t)\,dr. $$

Similarly, the total wing drag is

$$ D_{\text{wing}}(t)=2\int_0^b D'(r,t)\,dr. $$

However, lift and drag are defined relative to the local relative wind, not directly relative to the ground. Resolving them into vertical and forward components gives

$$ F_z'(r,t)=L'(r,t)\cos\gamma(r,t)-D'(r,t)\sin\gamma(r,t), $$ $$ F_x'(r,t)=L'(r,t)\sin\gamma(r,t)-D'(r,t)\cos\gamma(r,t). $$

The whole-bird force components are

$$ F_z(t)=2\int_0^b F_z'(r,t)\,dr, $$ $$ F_x(t)=2\int_0^b F_x'(r,t)\,dr. $$

The flight constraints are therefore

$$ \frac1T\int_0^T F_z(t)\,dt=mg, $$ $$ \frac1T\int_0^T F_x(t)\,dt=D. $$

7. Why flapping produces thrust

A fixed wing mainly produces lift perpendicular to the oncoming flow. A flapping wing changes the oncoming-flow direction. Therefore the aerodynamic force can tilt forward.

For small $\gamma$,

$$ \sin\gamma\approx \gamma\approx \frac{\dot h}{U}. $$

Ignoring drag for the moment,

$$ F_x'(r,t)\approx L'(r,t)\frac{\dot h(r,t)}{U}. $$

Thus the mean thrust depends on a correlation:

$$ \langle F_x\rangle \sim \left\langle L\frac{\dot h}{U}\right\rangle. $$
Thrust is not produced merely by moving the wing. It is produced by synchronizing lift production with the phase of motion that tilts the aerodynamic force forward.

8. Why the downstroke is usually dominant

$S_{\text{down}}$ = effective wing area during downstroke.

$S_{\text{up}}$ = effective wing area during upstroke.

Many birds extend the wing during downstroke and partially fold or twist it during upstroke, so

$$ S_{\text{down}}>S_{\text{up}}. $$

A rough cycle-averaged vertical force estimate is

$$ \langle F_z\rangle\approx \frac1T\left[ \int_{\text{downstroke}}\frac12\rho U_{\mathrm{eff}}^2S_{\text{down}}C_L\,dt + \int_{\text{upstroke}}\frac12\rho U_{\mathrm{eff}}^2S_{\text{up}}C_L\,dt \right]. $$

The upstroke is not irrelevant. It is often configured to reduce negative lift and reduce energetic cost.

9. Circulation: a deeper description of lift

$\boldsymbol{\omega}=\nabla\times\mathbf{u}$ = vorticity field.

$\Gamma(r,t)$ = bound circulation around the wing section at span $r$.

$C$ = closed curve surrounding the wing section.

$d\mathbf{l}$ = differential line element along $C$.

Circulation is defined as

$$ \Gamma=\oint_C \mathbf{u}\cdot d\mathbf{l}. $$

For a wing section in relative flow of speed $U_{\mathrm{eff}}$, the Kutta–Joukowski theorem gives

$$ L'(r,t)=\rho U_{\mathrm{eff}}(r,t)\Gamma(r,t). $$

For small-angle attached flow,

$$ \Gamma(r,t)\sim \pi c(r)U_{\mathrm{eff}}(r,t)\alpha_{\mathrm{eff}}(r,t). $$

Therefore,

$$ L'(r,t)\sim \pi\rho U_{\mathrm{eff}}(r,t)^2c(r)\alpha_{\mathrm{eff}}(r,t), $$

which recovers the thin-airfoil scaling.

The wing generates bound circulation; bound circulation generates lift.

10. Why a flapping wing must shed vortices

$D/Dt$ = material derivative following the fluid motion.

The material derivative is

$$ \frac{D}{Dt}=\frac{\partial}{\partial t}+\mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla. $$

In inviscid flow, Kelvin's circulation theorem states

$$ \frac{D\Gamma}{Dt}=0. $$

If a wing changes its bound circulation $\Gamma(r,t)$, the surrounding fluid must acquire compensating circulation. That compensating circulation appears as shed vorticity in the wake.

Time-varying wing circulation implies vortex shedding.

11. Momentum balance: pushing air downward and backward

$\Omega$ = control volume of air enclosing the bird and wake.

$\partial\Omega$ = boundary of the control volume.

$\mathbf{n}$ = outward unit normal on $\partial\Omega$.

$dV$ = differential volume element.

$dS$ = differential surface element.

The momentum balance for the air is

$$ \mathbf{F}_{\text{bird on air}} = \frac{d}{dt}\int_{\Omega}\rho\mathbf{u}\,dV + \int_{\partial\Omega}\rho\mathbf{u}(\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{n})\,dS + \int_{\partial\Omega}p\mathbf{n}\,dS. $$

By Newton's third law,

$$ \mathbf{F}_{\text{air on bird}}=-\mathbf{F}_{\text{bird on air}}. $$

Thus downward wake momentum produces lift, and backward wake momentum produces thrust.

12. Vortex impulse formulation

$\mathbf{I}(t)$ = hydrodynamic impulse of the vortex wake.

$\mathbf{x}$ = position vector.

$\boldsymbol{\omega}$ = vorticity field.

For an incompressible fluid,

$$ \mathbf{I}(t)=\frac{\rho}{2}\int_{\Omega}\mathbf{x}\times\boldsymbol{\omega}\,dV. $$

The aerodynamic force is related to the time rate of change of impulse:

$$ \mathbf{F}(t)\approx -\frac{d\mathbf{I}}{dt}. $$
The bird generates force by changing the vortex impulse of the wake.

13. Vortex-ring picture of slow flapping flight

$R$ = characteristic vortex-ring radius.

$\Gamma_{\text{ring}}$ = circulation of one vortex ring.

$f$ = wingbeat frequency.

$I_{\text{ring}}$ = impulse of one vortex ring.

The impulse of one vortex ring scales as

$$ I_{\text{ring}}\sim \rho\Gamma_{\text{ring}}\pi R^2. $$

If rings are produced at frequency $f$, the average force scales as

$$ \bar F\sim fI_{\text{ring}}. $$

Therefore,

$$ \bar F\sim \rho f\Gamma_{\text{ring}}R^2. $$

For weight support,

$$ mg\sim \rho f\Gamma_{\text{ring}}R^2. $$

14. Added mass: accelerating nearby air

$m_a'(r)$ = added mass per unit span.

For a wing section of chord $c(r)$,

$$ m_a'(r)\sim \rho c(r)^2. $$

The added-mass force per unit span scales as

$$ F_a'(r,t)\sim m_a'(r)\ddot h(r,t). $$

Thus,

$$ F_a'(r,t)\sim \rho c(r)^2\ddot h(r,t). $$

Since

$$ \ddot h(r,t)=-\omega^2H(r)\sin(\omega t), $$

the force magnitude scales as

$$ |F_a'|\sim \rho c(r)^2\omega^2H(r). $$
High-frequency flapping is expensive because acceleration scales like $\omega^2$.

15. Unsteady airfoil theory: Theodorsen model

$c$ = airfoil chord.

$b_c = c/2$ = airfoil semichord.

$h(t)$ = vertical displacement of the airfoil.

$\alpha(t)$ = pitch angle of the airfoil.

$a$ = dimensionless pitch-axis location relative to mid-chord.

$U$ = forward flight speed.

$k=\omega b_c/U$ = reduced frequency.

$H_0^{(2)}$ and $H_1^{(2)}$ = Hankel functions of the second kind.

Theodorsen's function is

$$ C(k)=\frac{H_1^{(2)}(k)}{H_1^{(2)}(k)+iH_0^{(2)}(k)}. $$

The lift per unit span is

$$ L' = \pi\rho b_c^2 \left( \ddot h+U\dot\alpha-b_c a\ddot\alpha \right) + 2\pi\rho U b_c C(k) \left[ U\alpha+\dot h+b_c\left(\frac12-a\right)\dot\alpha \right]. $$

This decomposes as

$$ L'=L'_{\text{added mass}}+L'_{\text{circulatory}}. $$

The first term is non-circulatory added-mass lift. The second term is circulatory lift. Real bird wings are finite, deformable, feathered, and often separated, so this is not a complete biological model; it is a mathematically clean reference model.

16. Dimensionless numbers

16.1 Reynolds number

$Re$ = Reynolds number.

$c$ = characteristic chord length.

$$ Re=\frac{\rho Uc}{\mu}=\frac{Uc}{\nu}. $$

It compares inertial and viscous effects. For birds, typically

$$ Re\sim 10^4-10^6. $$

16.2 Strouhal number

$St$ = Strouhal number.

$A$ = peak-to-peak flapping amplitude.

$$ St=\frac{fA}{U}. $$

Efficient flapping flight often lies near

$$ St\sim 0.2-0.4. $$

16.3 Reduced frequency

$k$ = reduced frequency.

Using chord $c$,

$$ k=\frac{\pi f c}{U}. $$

Equivalently, using semichord $b_c=c/2$,

$$ k=\frac{\omega b_c}{U}. $$

If $k\ll 1$, the flow is nearly quasi-steady. If $k=O(1)$, unsteady effects are essential.

17. Wing twist and spanwise variation

$V_x(r,t)$ = local forward component of relative airflow.

$V_z(r,t)$ = local vertical component of relative airflow.

$\alpha^*$ = target effective angle of attack.

The local effective angle of attack is

$$ \alpha_{\mathrm{eff}}(r,t) = \theta(r,t)-\tan^{-1}\left(\frac{V_z(r,t)}{V_x(r,t)}\right). $$

To keep $\alpha_{\mathrm{eff}}$ near a useful target $\alpha^*$, the wing should approximately satisfy

$$ \theta(r,t) \approx \alpha^*+\tan^{-1}\left(\frac{V_z(r,t)}{V_x(r,t)}\right). $$

This is why real bird wings twist spanwise. Different parts of the wing experience different relative velocities.

18. Aeroelasticity: the wing is deformable

$s$ = coordinate along the wing structure.

$y(s,t)$ = bending displacement.

$\rho_s$ = density of wing material.

$A_s$ = cross-sectional area of the wing structure.

$E$ = effective elastic modulus.

$I_s$ = second moment of area of the wing structure.

$f_{\text{aero}}(s,t)$ = aerodynamic force per unit length.

$f_{\text{muscle}}(s,t)$ = active muscular force per unit length.

A simplified beam equation is

$$ \rho_sA_s\frac{\partial^2y}{\partial t^2} + EI_s\frac{\partial^4y}{\partial s^4} = f_{\text{aero}}(s,t)+f_{\text{muscle}}(s,t). $$

This says:

$$ \text{structural inertia}+\text{elastic restoring force} = \text{aerodynamic forcing}+\text{muscular forcing}. $$

$Ca$ = aeroelastic Cauchy-like number.

$L$ = characteristic wing length.

A useful aeroelastic nondimensional number is

$$ Ca=\frac{\rho U^2L^3}{EI_s}. $$

Large $Ca$ means aerodynamic loads significantly deform the wing. Small $Ca$ means the wing is nearly rigid.

19. Power required for flapping flight

$P(t)$ = instantaneous mechanical power delivered to the air.

$\bar P$ = mean aerodynamic power over one wingbeat period.

$\eta$ = muscle efficiency.

The local mechanical power is force dot velocity. Using the full stress formulation,

$$ P(t)=\int_{S_w(t)}\left[\boldsymbol{\sigma}\mathbf{n}\right]\cdot\mathbf{V}_w\,dS. $$

The mean aerodynamic power is

$$ \bar P=\frac1T\int_0^T P(t)\,dt. $$

The metabolic power is approximately

$$ P_{\text{metabolic}}\approx \frac{\bar P}{\eta}. $$

$P_{\text{induced}}$ = power needed to generate lift by accelerating air downward.

$P_{\text{profile}}$ = power lost to drag on the wings.

$P_{\text{parasite}}$ = power lost to drag on the body.

$P_{\text{inertial}}$ = power associated with accelerating and decelerating the wings.

The mean aerodynamic power decomposes approximately as

$$ \bar P\approx P_{\text{induced}}+P_{\text{profile}}+P_{\text{parasite}}+P_{\text{inertial}}. $$

A useful induced-power scaling is

$$ P_{\text{induced}}\sim \frac{W^2}{\rho SU}. $$

$S_b$ = body frontal area.

$C_{D,\text{wing}}$ = wing drag coefficient.

$C_{D,\text{body}}$ = body drag coefficient.

Profile and parasite power scale approximately as

$$ P_{\text{profile}}\sim \rho S U^3 C_{D,\text{wing}}, $$ $$ P_{\text{parasite}}\sim \rho S_b U^3 C_{D,\text{body}}. $$

Thus a generic flight power curve has the form

$$ P(U)\sim \frac{A_1}{U}+A_2U^3+A_3, $$

where $A_1$, $A_2$, and $A_3$ are constants depending on body size, wing shape, air density, and flapping mechanics.

Low-speed flight is expensive because induced power is large. High-speed flight is expensive because drag power grows like $U^3$.

20. Final synthesis

The wing motion defines

$$ h(r,t),\qquad \theta(r,t). $$

These determine the local relative airflow:

$$ U_{\mathrm{eff}}(r,t),\qquad \alpha_{\mathrm{eff}}(r,t). $$

These determine local circulation and pressure:

$$ \Gamma(r,t),\qquad p(\mathbf{x},t). $$

These determine aerodynamic force:

$$ \mathbf{F}(t)=\int_{S_w(t)}\boldsymbol{\sigma}\mathbf{n}\,dS. $$

Because circulation changes in time, the wing sheds vortices. The vorticity field obeys

$$ \frac{\partial\boldsymbol{\omega}}{\partial t} + \mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla\boldsymbol{\omega} = \boldsymbol{\omega}\cdot\nabla\mathbf{u} + \nu\nabla^2\boldsymbol{\omega}. $$

The wake carries impulse:

$$ \mathbf{I}(t)=\frac{\rho}{2}\int \mathbf{x}\times\boldsymbol{\omega}\,dV. $$

The bird receives force roughly according to

$$ \mathbf{F}(t)\approx -\frac{d\mathbf{I}}{dt}. $$

The cycle-averaged constraints are

$$ \langle F_z\rangle=mg, $$ $$ \langle F_x\rangle=D. $$

The energetic cost is

$$ \bar P = \frac1T\int_0^T\int_{S_w(t)} \left[\boldsymbol{\sigma}\mathbf{n}\right]\cdot\mathbf{V}_w \,dS\,dt. $$
Birds fly by imposing time-dependent motion and twist on deformable finite wings, generating time-dependent circulation, shedding a structured vortex wake, and thereby transferring downward and backward momentum to the air. The reaction force supports weight and produces thrust.

The compact mathematical chain is

$$ h(r,t),\theta(r,t) \rightarrow \alpha_{\mathrm{eff}}(r,t) \rightarrow \Gamma(r,t) \rightarrow \boldsymbol{\omega}(\mathbf{x},t) \rightarrow \mathbf{I}(t) \rightarrow \mathbf{F}(t) \rightarrow \bar P. $$
