# The Physics of Bird Wing Flapping

*Unsteady aerodynamics, vortex dynamics, and aeroelasticity — no biology hand-waving.*

## 1. Governing equations: unsteady Navier–Stokes with moving boundaries

Let the air occupy a time-dependent domain $\Omega(t)$, excluding the bird body and wings.
The flow satisfies incompressible Navier–Stokes:

$$
\nabla \cdot \mathbf{u} = 0
$$

$$
\rho\left(\frac{\partial \mathbf{u}}{\partial t} + \mathbf{u} \cdot \nabla \mathbf{u}\right)
= -\nabla p + \mu \nabla^2 \mathbf{u}
$$

with no-slip / no-penetration on the moving wing surface $S_w(t)$:

$$
\mathbf{u}(\mathbf{x}, t) = \mathbf{V}_w(\mathbf{x}, t), \qquad \mathbf{x} \in S_w(t)
$$

The aerodynamic force on the bird is the surface integral of the Cauchy stress tensor
$\boldsymbol{\sigma} = -p\mathbf{I} + \mu(\nabla\mathbf{u} + \nabla\mathbf{u}^T)$:

$$
\mathbf{F} = \int_{S_w(t)} \boldsymbol{\sigma}\,\mathbf{n}\,dS
\approx -\int_{S_w(t)} p\,\mathbf{n}\,dS
$$

The pressure-dominance approximation holds because $Re \gg 1$ for birds.

---

## 2. Relevant nondimensional numbers

Bird flapping is **not** quasi-static. It is dominated by unsteady separated vortex dynamics.

**Reynolds number**

$$
Re = \frac{\rho U c}{\mu}
$$

Typical birds: $10^4 \lesssim Re \lesssim 10^6$. Inertia dominates viscosity, but viscosity
still sets boundary-layer separation and vortex shedding.

**Strouhal number**

$$
St = \frac{fA}{U}
$$

Efficient flapping flyers operate in $0.2 \lesssim St \lesssim 0.4$. This range means the wake
vortex spacing and flapping period are tuned so thrust is generated efficiently.

**Reduced frequency**

$$
k = \frac{\pi f c}{U}
$$

For birds, $k = O(0.1\text{–}1)$, so **unsteady effects are essential**. Quasi-steady
aerodynamics ($k \ll 1$) is inadequate.

## 3. Wing kinematics

A simplified wing section at spanwise coordinate $r$ has heaving motion:

$$
h(r,t) = H(r)\sin(\omega t), \qquad \omega = 2\pi f
$$

and pitching:

$$
\alpha(r,t) = \alpha_0(r) + \Theta(r)\sin(\omega t + \phi)
$$

The local velocity seen by the section:

$$
U_{\text{eff}}(r,t) = \sqrt{U^2 + \dot{h}(r,t)^2}
$$

The effective angle of attack — the central quantity in all that follows:

$$
\alpha_{\text{eff}}(r,t) = \alpha(r,t) - \tan^{-1}\!\left(\frac{\dot{h}(r,t)}{U}\right)
\approx \alpha - \frac{\dot{h}}{U}
$$

A flapping wing does not just move up and down; it continuously changes the aerodynamic
angle of attack as seen by each section.

---

## 4. Quasi-steady lift model

For a thin airfoil at moderate angle of attack, lift per unit span:

$$
L'(r,t) = \tfrac{1}{2}\rho\, U_{\text{eff}}^2\, c(r)\, C_L(\alpha_{\text{eff}})
$$

Thin-airfoil theory gives $C_L \approx 2\pi\alpha_{\text{eff}}$, so:

$$
L'(r,t) \approx \pi\rho\, U_{\text{eff}}^2\, c(r)\, \alpha_{\text{eff}}
$$

Total lift and level-flight constraint:

$$
L(t) = \int_0^b L'(r,t)\,dr, \qquad \langle L(t)\rangle = mg
$$

---

## 5. Thrust comes from tilting the aerodynamic force forward

During the downstroke, $\dot{h} < 0$, so the relative wind tilts downward by angle
$\gamma = \tan^{-1}(\dot{h}/U)$. The aerodynamic force tilts forward:

$$
T'(r,t) \approx F'(r,t)\,\frac{\dot{h}}{U}
$$

More carefully, the streamwise component of the pressure force is:

$$
T(t) = -\int_{S_w(t)} p\,n_x\,dS + \text{viscous terms}
$$

Geometrically: **the flapping wing redirects air downward and backward; the bird
receives lift and forward thrust by Newton's third law.**

---

## 6. Momentum theory: force equals rate of momentum imparted to air

For a control volume enclosing the bird:

$$
\mathbf{F}_{\text{bird on air}} = \frac{d}{dt}\int_\Omega \rho\,\mathbf{u}\,dV
+ \oint_{\partial\Omega} \rho\,\mathbf{u}(\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{n})\,dS
$$

In steady level flight, $mg \sim \rho S U w$ where $w$ is the induced downwash velocity.
Using actuator-disk scaling $w \sim \sqrt{mg/(2\rho S)}$:

$$
P_i \sim \frac{(mg)^{3/2}}{\sqrt{2\rho S}}
$$

This is why **large wing area reduces energetic cost**: the same momentum can be imparted
to more air at lower velocity.

---

## 7. Vorticity formulation: birds fly by sculpting vortex rings

Define vorticity $\boldsymbol{\omega} = \nabla \times \mathbf{u}$. Kelvin's theorem (inviscid):

$$
\frac{D\Gamma}{Dt} = 0
$$

Changes in wing-bound circulation $\Gamma(r,t)$ must be balanced by shed vorticity in the
wake — **that is why flapping flight leaves vortex loops**.

The Kutta–Joukowski theorem gives lift per span: $L' = \rho U\Gamma$.

The vortex impulse formula:

$$
\mathbf{I} = \frac{\rho}{2}\int \mathbf{x} \times \boldsymbol{\omega}\,dV
$$

$$
\mathbf{F} \approx -\frac{d\mathbf{I}}{dt}
$$

Wing flapping produces force by **changing the impulse of the wake vorticity** — this
is the deepest single-equation summary of bird flight.

---

## 8. Theodorsen model: unsteady thin-airfoil theory

For a harmonically pitching/heaving airfoil, classical unsteady aerodynamics gives:

$$
L' = \underbrace{\pi\rho b^2\left(\ddot{h} + U\dot{\alpha} - ba\ddot{\alpha}\right)}_{\text{added mass}}
+ \underbrace{2\pi\rho U b\,C(k)\left[\dot{h}/U + \alpha + b({\textstyle\frac{1}{2}} - a)\dot{\alpha}/U\right]}_{\text{circulatory}}
$$

where $b = c/2$, $a$ is the pitch-axis parameter, and $C(k)$ is Theodorsen's function:

$$
C(k) = \frac{H_1^{(2)}(k)}{H_1^{(2)}(k) + i H_0^{(2)}(k)}
$$

For birds this linear model is incomplete because they use high angles and separated
leading-edge vortices — but it captures the essential added-mass / circulatory split.

---

## 9. Added mass: accelerating air costs force

A wing does not merely move through air; it accelerates nearby air. For a section with
chord $c$, added-mass per unit span $m_a' \sim \rho c^2$.

For harmonic flapping $h = H\sin\omega t$:

$$
\ddot{h} = -\omega^2 H\sin\omega t
$$

$$
F_a' \sim \rho c^2 \omega^2 H
$$

This is why **high-frequency flapping becomes expensive** at small size: $F_a \propto f^2$.

---

## 10. Wingbeat power curve

Mean aerodynamic power:

$$
\bar{P} = \frac{1}{T}\int_0^T P(t)\,dt, \quad P(t) = \mathbf{F}(t)\cdot\mathbf{V}_{\text{wing}}(t)
$$

Component scalings:

$$
P_{\text{induced}} \sim \frac{W^2}{\rho S U}, \qquad
P_{\text{profile}} \sim \rho S U^3 C_{D,\text{wing}}
$$

$$
P_{\text{parasite}} \sim \rho S_b U^3 C_{D,\text{body}}, \qquad
P_{\text{inertial}} \sim I_w \omega^3 \Phi^2
$$

This gives the classic **U-shaped flight power curve**:

$$
P(U) \approx \frac{A}{U} + BU^3 + C
$$

Low speed is expensive (induced power). High speed is expensive (profile + parasite drag).
The minimum-power speed $U_{mp}$ is optimal for endurance; $U_{mr}$ (max range) is faster.

---

## 11. Why the downstroke dominates

During the **downstroke**: wing is fully extended ($S_{\text{down}} \approx S_{\max}$),
$C_L > 0$, and force has upward + forward components.

During the **upstroke**: many birds partially fold or twist the wing ($S_{\text{up}} < S_{\text{down}}$),
reducing negative lift and drag. Feathers can separate passively, creating slotted flow
and reducing upstroke drag dramatically.

Cycle-averaged lift constraint:

$$
\bar{L} \approx \frac{1}{T}\left[\int_{\text{down}} \tfrac{1}{2}\rho U_{\text{eff}}^2 S_{\text{down}} C_L\,dt
+ \int_{\text{up}} \tfrac{1}{2}\rho U_{\text{eff}}^2 S_{\text{up}} C_L\,dt\right] = mg
$$

## 12. Leading-edge vortices

At high angle of attack, attached-flow theory fails. The wing develops a **leading-edge
vortex (LEV)** — a region of strong spanwise vorticity that creates a low-pressure
suction peak:

$$
\Delta p \sim \rho\,\frac{\Gamma_{\text{LEV}}^2}{\ell^2}, \qquad
L_{\text{LEV}} \sim \rho U\,\Gamma_{\text{LEV}}\,b
$$

Birds stabilize LEVs via spanwise flow, wing twist, sweep, feather porosity, and
unsteady shedding. This is why a flapping wing can **exceed the static stall limit**
of a fixed wing — dynamic stall provides a brief burst of superlift.

---

## 13. Spanwise structure: local velocity increases toward the wingtip

For a wing rotating with angular velocity $\dot\phi$:

$$
V(r,t) \approx U + \dot\phi(t)\,r
$$

Dynamic pressure $q(r,t) = \tfrac{1}{2}\rho |\mathbf{V}_{\text{rel}}|^2$ grows with $r$,
so most aerodynamic force comes from the **outer wing**.

Torque about the shoulder:

$$
\tau(t) = \int_0^b r\,dF(r,t) \sim \int_0^b r\,\tfrac{1}{2}\rho V(r)^2 c(r) C_L\,dr
$$

Bird wings balance aerodynamic leverage against muscular and inertial cost.

---

## 14. Wing twist: solving the spanwise angle-of-attack problem

Because $V(r,t)$ varies with $r$, the required geometric twist to maintain $\alpha_{\text{eff}}$
near the optimal $\alpha^*$ is:

$$
\theta(r,t) \approx \alpha^* + \tan^{-1}\!\left(\frac{V_z(r,t)}{V_x(r,t)}\right)
$$

Real bird wings are **actively and passively deforming aeroelastic structures** that
achieve this twist through muscle actuation and feather compliance.

---

## 15. Aeroelasticity: the wing as a fluid–structure interaction system

Let the wing displacement be $\mathbf{y}(s,t)$. A simplified elastic bending model:

$$
\rho_s A\,\frac{\partial^2 \mathbf{y}}{\partial t^2} + EI\,\frac{\partial^4 y}{\partial s^4}
= f_{\text{aero}} + f_{\text{muscle}}
$$

The key dimensionless number — Cauchy number:

$$
Ca = \frac{\rho U^2 L^3}{EI}
$$

- Large $Ca$: aerodynamic loading strongly deforms the wing (feathers, membrane bats)
- Small $Ca$: wing behaves rigidly (large soaring birds)

Bird wings exploit **controlled compliance** — neither purely rigid nor passively floppy.

---

## 16. Wake topology

In slow flight the wake is a chain of **discrete vortex rings**. Each ring carries
downward momentum:

$$
I \sim \rho\,\Gamma\,\pi R^2
$$

If rings are shed at frequency $f$, the mean force scales as:

$$
\bar{F} \sim f\,I \sim \rho f\,\Gamma R^2, \qquad mg \sim \rho f\,\Gamma R^2
$$

For thrust, the rings are tilted so their impulse has a rearward component. In fast flight
the wake transitions to a continuous **reverse Kármán street** (ladder wake).

## 17. Minimal mathematical model

A usable reduced-order model (what the interactive demo above solves in real-time):

$$
h(t) = H\sin\omega t
$$

$$
\alpha(t) = \alpha_0 + \Theta\sin(\omega t + \phi)
$$

$$
U_{\text{eff}}(t) = \sqrt{U^2 + \dot{h}^2}
$$

$$
\alpha_{\text{eff}}(t) = \alpha(t) - \tan^{-1}\!\left(\frac{\dot{h}}{U}\right)
$$

$$
L(t) = \tfrac{1}{2}\rho S\,U_{\text{eff}}^2\,C_L(\alpha_{\text{eff}})
$$

$$
D(t) = \tfrac{1}{2}\rho S\,U_{\text{eff}}^2\,C_D(\alpha_{\text{eff}})
$$

Resolve into vertical/horizontal with $\gamma = \tan^{-1}(\dot{h}/U)$:

$$
F_z = L\cos\gamma - D\sin\gamma, \qquad F_x = L\sin\gamma - D\cos\gamma
$$

Cycle constraints: $\langle F_z\rangle = mg$, $\langle F_x\rangle = D_{\text{body}}$.

The chain:

$$
\boxed{\text{kinematics} \;\to\; \alpha_{\text{eff}} \;\to\; \Gamma(t) \;\to\; \text{vortex wake} \;\to\; \text{lift / thrust / power}}
$$

## 18. The deepest physical picture

Bird flight is **not** "wings push air down" in a simple static sense.

$$
\boxed{
\mathbf{F} \approx -\frac{d}{dt}\!\left[\frac{\rho}{2}\int \mathbf{x}\times\boldsymbol{\omega}\,dV\right]
}
$$

Birds fly by **imposing time-dependent circulation on a deforming finite wing**, thereby
shedding a structured vortex wake whose impulse equals the aerodynamic force.

The design problem solved by evolution is:

$$
\min_{\text{wing shape, kinematics, muscles}} \bar{P}
\quad \text{subject to} \quad
\langle F_z\rangle = mg, \quad \langle F_x\rangle = D, \quad \alpha_{\text{eff}}(r,t) < \alpha_{\text{stall}},
\quad \text{structural constraints}
$$

That is the rigorous physics: unsteady viscous incompressible flow, deforming-boundary
circulation control, and wake impulse dynamics — solved approximately by every bird on
every wingbeat.
